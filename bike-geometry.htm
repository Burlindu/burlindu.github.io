I like basic 'human' numbers - the length between the grips and the seat, and the angles of the head and seat tube.

My favorites are the 'Human Number' (I made that up), which is the above mentioned distance between the middle of the grip and the middle of the seat.

And the 'Burl #', which is how forward you are over the pedals. A Burl number of zero is when the bottom bracket is exactly 1/2 way between the grips and the seat. I like a slack seat tube, equal to my head tube angle. So I think almost all bikes these days have positive (like over 100mm) Burl #'s; which for me is too far forward.

My theory is that racing/speed/power cyclists, who dominate both 'bike commentary' and 'bike design for manufacturing' like the bb back and the Burl # high because for power/speed/racing (or pretending to be racing) the arms and grips are forward and are used for leverage of the body and leg weight. It's harder on your hands (like Grant / Rivendell says, too much weight on the hands) - because the mass isn't over the pedals going straight down - it's AHEAD of the pedals and being LEVERAGED, with your hands as the pivot, downward. The hands, instead of just being a 'light guidance' tool, bear the brunt of being the pivot point that the body mass is leveraged against over the pedals. Great for power. Crappy for Burl, who wants to float through the downtown and steer his bike with one finger if he so desires. The bike is long, to get you low and forward, and the BB is back (sometimes beding the seat tube to get it as far back as possible) to increase the leverage of the forward body over the pedal. The Burl riding position has the Human on the seat, using the legs to power. The modern 'BB forward' design means transferring the emphasis away from teh seat to the legs and pedals. Again, goof for power, but Burl is just cruising, and he wants power-on-demand, not max-power-at-all-times. You hands can't take direct weight from teh rider - but they can act as a pivot point (stress point) for leveraging the weight of teh rider into pedalling. The race rider wants that maximum pedalling and so the leverage position is good. I want the least stress on my hands, so I want to be upright with the pedals more in front of my bum, so the stress quite deliberatly does not have much to do with my hands, which are for steering, drinking from my cup, or waving at a driver who waited instead fo cutting off the bike lane.

Or.... I ride like a Flaneur (upright, looking around, going moderate pace) so my c-of-mass is over or behind the BB, with less stress on the hands and more on the pedals and bum.
