Burl's White Paper on Bicycle Ergonomics
2025
(1)
Bicycle size depends on 2 things:
Length and Height

(2)
These 2 have to be alterable independatlly, as a person with shorter arms or torso or wanting to sit more upright may require a shorter bike.
A person with longer legs or wanting a racier body position may require a taller bike.

(3)
Changing the bike length should not force the user to alter the height. Altering the height should not force an altreration of the length.
Racing position means leaning forward which lengthens the bike.
City position is upright, shortening the bike.

Slacker head tubes makes for more control and steadiness in the turns (shrper is quicker).

Slack seat tube makes for a more relaxed pedaling position. *** Often riders have the seat too far forward, moving the rider very for forward over the pedal, simpley because the bike is too long (ie racing length framme on a casual rider)
Seat tube and Head tube should be the same angle so that raising or lowering these 2 keeps them the same distance apart (when measuring at level,. There should be no length penalty for raising or lowering teh seat or the bars.
Step Through Ladies' bikes

A postulate that the idea that a Ladies' bike has a lower crossbar is developed from the wonderful idea of making all bikes with a crazy high crossbar and then not making small bikes for small people. And women are on average smaller than men.
In my vast experience of having a groin I would say that a man also, in addition to the feelings of the ladies, does not like to hit their groin area on a crossbar. However, if you design most bikes so that a levelish crossbar will hit the groin of people under 5'8, and way more women than men are under 5'8, than suddenly you will have to make a 'ladies' bike with a lower crossbar or a step-through design. It's not a question of gender; it's a question of bike manufacturers just contyinually making bikes with a high crossbar - and oversize frames and wheels -like it was still the olden days, which it is not.
