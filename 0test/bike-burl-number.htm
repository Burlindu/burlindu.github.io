Burl Number
The Burl Number

is how forward (or back) the pedalling center
(the bottom bracket)

is from the human center
(mid-way between the grips and the bum area of the seat)

-----------------------------------------

In the sample image the BURL NUMBER for the GLIDER is minus 9, so the rider (center of mass) is pedalling just behind the BB, almost right on top.
It means the seat & head are slack & the rider position is relaxed, like on an old dutch town bike. The rider is furthur back & more upright for visibility & comfort/endurance over speed.

the Peugeot rider - on a road bike but on the close/relaxed grips - has a BURL NUMBER of plus 115.
So that rider's center-of-mass is ahead of the bottom bracket by 115mm - More pressure on the hands, lower head position and more forward over the pedals for power over endurance.

-----------------------------------------

Clearly the GLIDER rider is stitting upright & the pedals are well in front;
The PEUGEOT road bike rider is bent much furthur over and more up over the pedals.
The GLIDER rider gets power from the mid-body and has visibility (upright position) and grip control (C of mass and therefore leverage closer to the grips) at a premium - over power - for safety in city traffic.


[[[ NOTES ]]]
Modern mountain bikes have short rear stays and crazy shallow seat tube angles (effective) for power climbing and agility in super-fast downhills; a city bike is riding mostly level-ish at 10-20 km/h - modern mountain bike geometry is precisely designed for a rider pretty much never going at that speed & terrain. Road bikes of course are fast on roads but too fast for regular city use - and even modern road bikes have come to start using tires over 3/4" / 20mm. But wih short stays and forward position - and drop bars - road bikes are stilld esigned for speed and power, which is not my goal. The mountain bike steepens the seat tube to move the riider center of mass forward; the road bike move the grips forward & down for teh same effect. I want the opposite: seat back, grips level with seat, grips back in line with the fork.

It could also be noted that mountain bikes shorted the stem (to nothing) at the same time as offsetting the seat tube from the bottom bracket - so the bike length is still long, but the seat can move back AND the bottom bracket too! That's why they have so much design effort now down where the stays meet the bottom bracket. I want to avoid all that effort by agreeing to the short stem but just letting the bike shorten and keeping a conventional (and attractive) triangle.

Some town bike use a crazy swept back bar so the grip is behind - sometimes well behind - the axis of the fork. This shortens the rider area, like I want, and it kicks the front wheel way out from so you can use big wheels and fenders and still have toe clearance. Again, I'd prefer to keep it simple. Use 650b wheels and again - keep the bike geometry/stance conventional without need for crazy bars and a super long bike. Very big wheels are make tire clearance at the back harded and raise up the back rack, where we'd like to keep loads lower.

So I'm avoiding the low but hard aero stance of road drop bars, avoiding the scrunched bottom bracket - stay area of mountain bikes and going back to smaller (650b) wheels so you don't have to keep jigging the bars or the top tube of whatever to get the cleance you need for fenters, toes and 2" tires. The goal is to make it all easy and to make it look easy - like a plain triangles of an old Ritchey or Bridgestone frame.

The Ritchey/Bridgestones were the nicest looking old/short cross-country MTB's; road bikes are the most elegent bikes for speed. A Trek Supercaliber is the best looking modern downhill bike (even if it's classified as cross crountry, which in modern terms means hills with a ski lift). A Rivendell is the proper modern town bike, but they are prone to too swept back bars and the bard to high - one a means of artifically shortening teh bike (grips behind the fork axis) and the other an artuifical means of shortening the bike (grips up high like you're a bus driver). Oversize head tubes are also a problem - they raise bothe the grips andespecially the top tube while giving abosolutely nothing in return, and then a lot of work has to be done to shrik the height back down for user. It's usless extra work.
[[[ END NOTES ]]]


Human Number
The Human number is the distance from mid-seat to mid-hand grips
The top tube length is nice, but the tube angles, stem & bars, & seat position means it does not represent the actual rider position.


Bike Size & Style
So the Human Number will give you the length of your riding position
- the most important actual number for bike riding (bike fit)

While the Burl number will tell you what style of bike you have
- The more positive the Burl Number the more 'race-like' your bike
- The more negative the Burl Number the more 'vintage-town' and relaxed your bike
