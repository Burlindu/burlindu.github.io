Geometry
I like basic 'human' numbers - the length between the grips and the seat, and the angles of the head and seat tube.

My favorites are the 'Human Number' (I made that up), which is the above mentioned distance between the middle of the grip and the middle of the seat.

And the 'Burl #', which is how forward you are over the pedals. A Burl number of zero is when the bottom bracket is exactly 1/2 way between the grips and the seat. I like a slack seat tube, equal to my head tube angle. So I think almost all bikes these days have positive (like over 100mm) Burl #'s; which for me is too far forward.

My theory is that racing/speed/power cyclists, who dominate both 'bike commentary' and 'bike design for manufacturing' like the bb back and the Burl # high because for power/speed/racing (or pretending to be racing) the arms and grips are forward and are used for leverage of the body and leg weight. It's harder on your hands (like Grant / Rivendell says, too much weight on the hands) - because the mass isn't over the pedals going straight down - it's ahead of the pedals and being levereged, with your hands as the pivot, downward. The hands, instead of just being a 'light guidance' tool, bear the brunt of being the pivot point that the body mass is leveraged against over the pedals. Great for power. Crappy for Burl, who wants to float through the downtown and steer his bike with one finger if he so desires.

The bike is long, to get you low and forward, and the BB is back (sometimes beding the seat tube to get it as far back as possible) to increase the leverage of the forward body over the pedal. The Burl riding position has the Human on the seat, using the legs to power. The modern 'BB back' design means transferring the emphasis away from the seat to the legs and pedals. Again, good for power, but Burl is just cruising, and he wants power-on-demand, not max-power-at-all-times. You hands can't take direct weight from the rider - but they can act as a pivot point (stress point) for leveraging the weight of the rider into pedalling. The race rider wants that maximum pedalling and so the leverage position is good. I want the least stress on my hands, so I want to be upright with the pedals more in front of my bum, so the stress quite deliberatly does not have much to do with my hands, which are for steering, drinking from my cup, or waving at a driver who waited instead fo cutting off the bike lane.


Or.... I ride like a Flaneur (upright, looking around, going moderate pace) so my seat is back and the stress is divided between the seat and the pedals, with hands free. My pedal direction in forward - which I suspect Bike Fit James would measure as producing less pedalling power.

The racing position, so far forward/low, gets the legs right over the pedals for increased power, but also forces the hands to bear the stress of steadying the forward body which is no longer balanced over top of the seat and pedals.

Burl the flaneur is a lazier rider - he pushed forward with the pedal stroke, not raising his body off the seat. The stress of holding the body up is maintained by the body core, leaving the hands loose and free.

41
I just read Rivendell Reader (RR41) issue 41 - Grant had said on the Rivendell site that the Top Tube length myth was settled here.

They have a sort of Burl # - it's 'Saddle Setback' from the BB. Great!

The overall tone of the article is a bit baffling though. It says that top tube length does not matter as much as people say because riders have to reach furthur for lower grips and moving back the seat also increases length. But heck, that seems pretty bloody obvious. If you don't know if you want a racing position & don't know if you want the seat forward or back then you'd have no business doing frame design anyway, or even picking a bike frame, so it sounds like just talk. Of course top tube length doesn't matter if you don't pay attention to rider-grip-seat position. But if you pay no attention to those things, are aren't riding. So it sounds like an arguement against nothing.

We size a bike knowing how much seat setback we like - ie setapost angle - and where we want the grips. And we have some idea of what handlebars we like and how long a stem - ie how much the grip is offset from the fork (along the HT angle). We know what we want from these things. So what's left? It's the top tube length. If you get the wrong top tube length you have to mess up the grip postion - the handling - and/or the seat position - the pedaling - to get the right length. And you do need the right length. So the top tube matters a lot. We know if we want to ride bent over and fast or upright and slow - and if you have the wrong size top tube you are going to have to compromise your preferred riding position. And the frame is the 'hardest to change later' part. So it's super important.

RR41 seems to be saying that you are over estimating the importance of top tube length if you ignore grip and seat position. Well, no offense, but DUH. I would say that RR41 undersells the importance of top tube length, because it stresses bike length being according to seat and grip position. Well, the frame matters. I know Grant says somewhere most riders ride too small, which surprises me (I would say the opposite). I think he's tilting at windmills from decades back - some kind of old-school deal where he's making sit-up bikes and fighting frame design that is built around low race positions that get their length of position from riders down on the road low aero position. So he's saying (I'm guessing) that riders need bigger frame sizes because they are positioning for his (shorter) upright position, not for longer (road racing) position. Maybe? In our reality, now, people are widely using frames that are too big because racers sit low and forward and so use long frames, and town riders sit upright and relaxed, and need smaller, shorter bikes.

Bike Fit James, in 'Proof of Sweat', seemed to see what I see - riders get bikes that are - or are set up - too long. Anyway, RR41's SEAT SETBACK is a great #, although I don't need it because I just use the seat tube angle and know I can slide the seat to make that steeper or slacker if I want. If you have your seatpost setback # you need the other corresponding number as well - the handlebar set-forward. Then you have your Burl #! Instead of 2 numbers I want just one, so instead of a seat-to-BB # and a Grip-to-BB # I use one number - distance from seat-to-grip-C to BB. It sounds convoluted, but if you're into it, I think it really says something about the bike - wether it's an XS or Xl frame.

That second RR41 page seems a bit crazy - it appears to claim the rider distance shortens, on the same Top Tube fame, if the seat tube angle changes. Ha ha ha no it does not. The distance forward from the BB changes, but the rider reach does not. So changing the seat tube angle changes foot position, not bike length. What a wierd error. You need to get your bike the right length, AND get a frame style that has the BB more forward or more back, depending on your preference.

Charts
[Chart above is from Geometry Geeks website, as an example]

These diagrams are great except they give you everything relative to frame size and not rider size.

Reach, from the BB to the top of the head tube, is great if you are trying figure how big a triangle you are trying to weld together. But different riders have the grip in totally different positions, so you can't see what reach you need (a frame size) from this diagram. You need the grip location - and the seat location - for rider length. So these diagrams show you what a manufacturer needs to know, not a rider. I love the diagrams - they are great and standardized - but they sure leave you a lot of work to do if you are trying to see what size of frame to order to get a bike you like.

A proper modern bike geometry chart - seeing as they accompany fully built bikes - would be a chart fo each bike size, with grip and seat shown, with the seat at suggested height for a rider for that size bike.
