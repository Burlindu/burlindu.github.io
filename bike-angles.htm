Angles

Slack Angles are around 70° or less

Classic Roaster bikes - and the early Tom Richey (above) and Joe Breeze Bikes - were slack in both the head and seat tubes.

Modern (road/utility) bikes tend to run less slack angles areound 72°

Modern MTB bikes are totally different from my early Kona & Breezer - which were off-road cross country style. Dual suspension modern MTB are designed primarily for downhilling - so the head tube is VERY slack & teh seat tube is VERY upright. The upright seat tube is becaue the rider may eb seated uphill and wants the body way far forward - unlike flat level rising. The slack head tube is for control - a kicked out fork is more stable (so vintage ducth city bikes also have a very slack head tube). SO.... the trend of making basic modern bikes having steep seat tubes is dumb for utility/commuter riders - who need good performance at near level grade.

Burl's Angle Theory

(1)

Make the head tube angle the same as the seat tube angle. This keeps the bike length - and so general performance profile - the same no matter how high or low you mount the seat or stem.

(2)

Use slack angles, like 69°. Some vintage bikes go lower, like 67°. The slack head tube gives smoother control on the forks - good for both crazy speeds on a downhill (MTB) or arcing around streets in the city. Also use a slack (same angle) on the seat tube. High performace racing may call for steep seat tube angles, but comfortable, upright happy town biking is much better with the cranks rotating around ahead of the seat and hips. Note that Rivendell says also that long chain stays have a noticable positive effect on ride feel (& downhill bikes now bend the tubes to tuck the chainring into the real wheel - the opposite of the Rivendell long bike).



Summary

Vintage town / cruiser / sportster bikes:
Slack Head & Slack Seat

Classic MTB (90's 26" wheels)
Moderatly Slack Head & Seat

Modern Hybrid / Town / Utility (base MTB) bikes:
Median (72°) angled head tube, moderatly steep seat tube Angle

Modern MTB (downhill) bikes:
Slack Head tubes, Steep Steat tubes

Burl wants:
Slack Head & Slack Steat, at same angles



Modern bike design is sad, because the under $1000 bikes take design cues from downhill MTB's (which have stupidly steep seat tubes) or race-style road bikes, which are too long.

Bike design for flat (pavement or dirt) should have slack head and seat tubes and be short-ish.

The reason hipsters like old 90's mountain bikes is the smaller wheel size make toe and tire clearance easier (& fits in elevator easier) and the tube angles are better, and the shorter top tubes make them comfortable for every-day riding. Super slack angles from vintage Raleighs combined with short geometry & smaller wheels (26 or 650b) from classic MTB's are a good model for a modern city bike - but of course with a modern 1x drivetrain (or internal geared hub).

Higher performace bikes (higher $$) can be longer and / or steeper seat angles. Or slack beauties like Rivendell's. That's it.

* Note I like small vintage bend-back bars that align the grip with the head tube. A modern headset & stem could allow a stem of several inches and a U-shaped bar, for grips aligned with the head tube and lots of straight bar for gear mounting and grips etc.
